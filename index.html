Climate Change and Wildlife Impacts in Australia

Exploring the environmental and ecological impacts of climate change

Emissions in Australia

Over the years, there was a rapid increase in CO2 emission in Australia. CO2 is very prominent as it makes up approximately 74.5% of all global greenhouse gas emissions annually. This is depicted by the steep incline of the line graph with its peak at around 2006. Though it is declining, it contributes heavily towards climate change. The electricity (burning of fossil fuels to generate electricity) and transport sector play crucial role in the production of greenhouse gases including CO2.

Mean Temperature Anomaly in Australia

This choropleth map illustrates the Mean Temperature Anomaly (°C) across the states of Australia, showcasing the effect of global climate change. Driven primarily by the steady rise of atmospheric CO2 and other greenhouse gases, global temperatures have risen significantly, which is clearly reflected in this map as all the states have an orange shade which correlates to an increased temperature anomaly.

Mean Temperature Anomaly over the Years

This bar graph shows Australia's Mean Temperature Anomaly (°C) from 1960 through 2025, providing a timeline of how the climate has shifted over the last several decades. In the 1960s and 1970s, blue bars (representing cooler than average years) were highly frequent. However, after the mid-1980s, orange bars (representing hotter than average years) are prominent with the temperature anomaly gradually increasing. The black line shows the average temperature anomaly and we can see that it inclines towards an increased temperature anomaly.
